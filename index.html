About

Hello and welcome to my homepage!

I am a recent PhD graduate in Physics at Heidelberg University.
My research interests are the evolution of massive stars, core-collapse supernovae and gravitational wave astrophysics. One of the main tools used in my research is supervised machine learning.

During the PhD, I have been a member of the Physics of Stellar Objects group at the Heidelberg Institute for Theoretical Studies and a fellow of the International Max Planck Research School for Astronomy and Cosmic Physics. Research

NEUTRON STAR AND BLACK HOLE FORMATION FROM IRON CORE-COLLAPSE:
  1. Which stars succeed and which fail to explode?

    Massive stars undergoing iron core-collapse (CC) at the end of their evolution terminate their lives either in successful or failed supernovae (SNe). Failure of the neutrino-driven SN mechanism results in black hole formation by direct collapse. Its success leads to a SN explosion, leaving behind either a neutron star or a black hole. In this work, we formulate explodability criteria that allow us to predict the final fates of massive stars at the onset of iron-core infall. We use these criteria and a set of single and binary-stripped star models to derive a CCSN recipe based on global stellar parameters that are determined already at the end of core-helium burning. It therefore is applicable for rapid binary population synthesis and other studies requiring the prediction of SN outcomes at scale.
    doi:10.1051/0004-6361/202554931

    The code for predicting the final fates of stars and discriminating their compact remnant types based on the pre-SN properties using the explodability and fallback-BH formation criteria is publicly available in form of a Jupyter Notebook (in Python) via Zenodo. In another Notebook, you can find the code implementing the CCSN recipe that is based on global parameters, applicable at the end of core-helium burning.

GRAVITATIONAL WAVE ASTRONOMY OF HYPERMASSIVE NEUTRON STARS:
  1. Gravitational waves from neutron star merger remnants

    The merger of two neutron stars (NS) results either in prompt collapse to a black hole or in a quasi-stable hypermassive neutron star (HMNS). The HMNS is supported by differential rotation, thermal effects and repulsive nuclear forces, and continues to emit gravitational waves (GWs) until it either stabilizes or undergoes a delayed collapse to a black hole. One of the ways to detect GWs on Earth is the matched-filtering technique, which requires efficient GW template models. These are scanned through the interferometry data in order to dig GW signals out of a noisy background. In this work, we run numerical relativity simulations of neutron star mergers at variable total mass up to the threshold mass for prompt collapse. We then use this data for supervised learning to fit a time-domain model for the GWs emitted by HMNSs that remain quasi-stable over at least 17 ms, for two different fixed Equations of State: APR4 and SFHX. The model is fast and accurate enough to be used for real signal searches at advanced LIGO sensitivity.
    doi:10.1103/PhysRevD.111.023002

    You can download the gravitational wave model for the APR4 Equation-of-State from GitLab and test it out using a simple Python script.

EVOLUTION OF MASSIVE STARS:
  1. Efficient stellar evolution forecasting during >99.9% of stellar lifetimes

    Many astrophysical studies require efficient but reliable predictive models of stellar evolution. Examples are stellar N-body dynamics, large-scale galactic evolution simulations, iterative optimization-based stellar parameter inference and rapid population synthesis. In this work, we use supervised machine learning methods to construct a model predicting stellar evolution from the zero-age-main-sequence up to the end of core-helium burning over a mass range from red dwarves to the most massive Wolf-Rayet stars. The deep-learning model is trained on evolutionary tracks pre-computed with the detailed stellar evolution code MESA, and acquires the capability to generalize predictions over a continuous parameter space. It casts millions of predictions within tens of seconds on a 4-core CPU, while keeping the predictive errors orders of magnitude below typical observational uncertainties.
    doi:10.1051/0004-6361/202347118

    The fitted stellar evolution emulator models are publicly available in form of a Jupyter Notebook (in Python) via Zenodo. In the same release, you find the code and demo of the Hierarchical Nearest-Neighbor Interpolation (HNNI) algorithm: it is an alternative solution to automated stellar track interpolation up to the end of core-helium burning that we develop, which however needs continued access to a stellar evolution catalog and is slower.
